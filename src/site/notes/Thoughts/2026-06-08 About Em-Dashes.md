## A Rant About Em-Dashes and (by extension) AI

I *like* em-dashes.

There. I said it. I've been using em-dashes forever. Pretty much ever since I first encountered them in some sci-fi or fantasy novel as a teenager. They're useful. Particularly to a neurodivergent brain like mine that tends to inject interjections, asides, and unnecessary context regularly. (I got in trouble for this all the time in uni.) When I'm not using em-dashes, I'm using parentheses. (See? Better get used to this. It's a pattern.) And parallel structure. Oh, and sometimes I like to use “it's not x, it's y,” too. (Though, okay, I'm starting to eliminate that one. A girl's gotta draw a line somewhere.)

So, imagine my frustration when someone looks at my work and says, “Oh! You clearly used AI to compose this. Look at all the em-dashes!” 

Um. No. No, I didn't. So. You know… *Get lost, dude*.

More to the point: Like it or hate it, ethical or not, AI was trained on data scraped from the internet and scanned from decades of literature. This means it's absorbed human grammar and sentence structure. And many of the best writers in history used—and continue to use—em-dashes. While I don't claim to be among their number, I refuse to give up my right to use the full breadth of punctuation, convention, counter-convention, or any other expression in my efforts to communicate via the written word just because a probability algorithm trained on human language and writing uses it too. This means I refuse to give up my right to use em-dashes.

Look, I get it. AI-generated slop is a problem—on many levels. The noise around it is loud, and for good reason. Its output is derivative in both construction and content. (Of *course* it is; everything it outputs is derived from everything it was trained on. This shouldn't be a surprise, people. Humans are derivative, too. We just happen to derive our output from choices based on our lived experiences, rather than statistical probabilities.) Its patterns have become clear simply because they repeat so often. Humans are pattern-seers, too.

That said, according to Jason Hamilton[^1], creator of the [Nerdy Novelist](https://youtube.com/@thenerdynovelist) YouTube channel, an editor he spoke to said that, when her publication ran a contest open to AI-generated or assisted submissions, the technical polish on the language of the submissions was high, but the underlying craft (plot structure, character arcs, motivation and themes, etc.) was lacking[^2]. (I wish I could tell you the name of the editor, but he never gives it. He does mention she worked for [Mythulu Magazine](https://mythulu.com), but their website no longer works. The only external reference I found to confirm its existence was at the [Community of Literary Magazines and Presses website](https://www.clmp.org/readers/publisher/mythulu/). So, take that as you will.)

Clearly, Hamilton's a fan of AI. His whole channel is based on helping writers use AI in their work. I've watched several of his videos. He often asserts that AI can be a useful tool in a writer's toolkit, but that it's not a replacement for learning the actual craft of writing and narrative[^3]. I think he's right about that. But I don't advocate for wholesale adoption of his framework or ideas—mostly because he and I write very differently. (He's comfortable letting AI compose his prose; I'm not.) That… and I don't actually *trust* AI. It hallucinates and, in its desire to please the user, often tells us what we want to hear rather than what may be true.

I'm not alone. Many people don't trust AI. It seems to be the prevailing sentiment these days. I'm just not rabid about it. I prefer a more nuanced discussion. Which means separating the issue of the initial training practices in AI development from the use of current AI tools, now several generations beyond those initial models.

Were those initial training methods problematic? Absolutely. Are current training methods problematic? Possibly. Possibly not. Frankly, I haven't researched enough about the current state of LLM development to know the nuances of that particular discussion, and it's not something I want to delve into here. I *do* know that there are efforts underway to reform such practices, which may—who knows?—lead to some form of restitution or protections for those affected by the early unethical training practices. That needs to continue. 

But the current atmosphere of paranoia that makes anti-AI targets out of writers just because of superficial grammatical styling is, quite frankly, demeaning. “Your work is polished, therefore you must be using AI,” is both reductionist and insulting. I've spent decades honing my skill. This doesn't mean everything I write is gold; a lot of it isn't. But if it sucks, it's either because we have wildly differing tastes or opinions about things (and I can't do anything about that); or it's because I haven't put in the effort to make it better (*that*, I can fix).

Alice Delormes Benites is the Professor of Human-Machine-Communication and Co-head of the Institute of Translation and Interpreting at ZHAW, Winterthur in Switzerland. According to her [TEDx bio](https://youtu.be/KOLEKtiZjg8):

> Her research focuses on the adoption of new technologies for language work.
> 
> She has been investigating how machine translation and large language models are being used by language professionals and by the broad audience and seeks to find out what the different user groups need to know in order to make the best use of new technologies. Her motto: technology is always about people.

She advocates *educated* use of AI. (The emphasis is mine, and intentional.) And she outlines both the advantages and limitations of the technology. It's a tool, but it's only useful if you have the underlying expertise in your field to use it properly. You have to understand the craft behind what you're trying to create. It simply can't take you all the way to your destination. Not yet, anyway. Maybe that'll change.

But I hope not.

Nevertheless, this is why AI-generated images and stories usually fall flat. The AI recognizes and uses patterns, so it's no surprise it can mimic styles and frameworks used by experienced creators. What the AI can't do is *care*. There are a million subtle decisions master storytellers and artists use to evoke a response in the audience. They care about their work, there's intent behind what they say, and they're invested in how it's received once it's released.

AI can't do that—care about the work, create with intent, or invest in the outcome. It doesn't suffer ego, and it isn't going to be crushed whenever we reject its output in favour of something else. In fact, it can't create anything without a human at a keyboard telling it what to do. (I'd say it can't even publish anything it created without human intervention, but the rise of agentic AI and its recent adventures with things like Moltbook and OpenClaw blur that reality.[^4])

But, here's the thing: AI *can* be useful. There are some tasks it excels at—most of them tedious and repetitive (which, not gonna lie, I'll happily outsource any time I can). It's great at collating data, analyzing structure and patterns, checking for consistency. Just remember: *Garbage in, garbage out.* It makes a pretty decent research assistant, if you know how to direct it and are willing to put the effort into fact checking its results; you can't rely on its conclusions, but double-checking its work can help you reach your own. It even makes a good sounding board, simply because the act of explaining your thoughts to it requires you to compose and clarify them to yourself first.

And that's where the gold is found. If I'm going to use AI—*when* I use AI—it'll be to encourage myself to think critically and clearly. To challenge my assumptions. To see the other guy's point of view, so I can be sure I'm not writing straw men into my work, sure what I want to say has a chance to resonate. It *won't* be to spit out words that may, but likely won't, capture what I want to say or, more importantly, *how* I want to say it.

I choose every em-dash I add to what I write. I decide every metaphor, simile, and parallel structure I want. Not some sophisticated, predictive algorithm. *Me.* Sometimes, I do it right. Sometimes, I don't. But that just means I'm human.

So, thank god for that.
